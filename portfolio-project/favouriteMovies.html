The Best Movies According to Gustavo

This a very simple, first steps project where I listed my 3 favourite movies
(actually 5, if you count the LOTR trilogy separately). Hope you like it!

Pulp Fiction

More than just a film, Pulp Fiction feels like a secret club you’re lucky enough to be in. It’s that album you know by heart, where every line, every song, and every scene shift feels like a personal touchstone. This isn't just a movie I watch; it's one I visit.

Quentin Tarantino’s 1994 landmark work reassembled the DNA of cinema with a reckless, brilliant confidence. By chopping up three crime stories and stitching them back together out of order, he did something magical: he made us focus not on what happens, but on how it happens. We already know Butch (Bruce Willis) survives his ordeal, so the tension in the pawn shop basement becomes not about survival, but about the sheer, horrific experience. It’s a film that trusts its audience completely.

What I adore, and what makes it feel so intimate upon every rewatch, is the glorious contradiction at its heart. These are hitmen discussing the ethics of a foot massage and the virtues of a "Royale with Cheese" moments before a brutal execution. It’s a boxer listening to "Misirlou" before betraying a crime lord, clinging to his father’s watch. It’s the terrifying, almost slapstick horror of an adrenaline needle to the heart. The dialogue isn't just cool; it’s the soul of the film, revealing the humanity (or lack thereof) in these characters.

The performances are etched into cinematic history. John Travolta’s charming, weary Vincent Vega, Samuel L. Jackson’s biblical and terrifying Jules, and Uma Thurman’s radiant, dangerously playful Mia—they don’t feel like characters, they feel like people.

For me, Pulp Fiction is the definition of timeless. Its razor-sharp writing, iconic soundtrack, and breathtaking style never get old. It’s a film that made me fall in love with the art of storytelling, and it remains, without a doubt, one of my all-time favourites.

Guardians of the Galaxy vol.1

Before 2014, the idea of a talking raccoon and a sentient tree becoming one of the most emotionally resonant duos in cinema seemed absurd. But that’s the magic of Guardians of the Galaxy. James Gunn didn’t just make a superhero movie; he crafted a spectacularly weird, heartfelt letter to the loners, the losers, and anyone who has ever found their family in the most unexpected place.

On the surface, it’s a cosmic romp—a rollicking adventure with spaceship chases and a quest for a powerful orb. But at its core, this is a story about a boy who ran away abducted by aliens, still grieving for his mother, and the fellow misfits he collects along the way. They are a thief, an assassin, a rage-fueled brute, a genetically engineered raccoon, and a tree that only speaks three words. They shouldn’t work, but they do, spectacularly.

What makes this film so deeply personal to me is its perfect balance of humor and heart. The comedy is sharp and character-driven, from Rocket’s endless scheming to Drax’s literal-minded brilliance. Yet, the film never shies away from its emotional weight. Peter Quill listening to his "Awesome Mix" tape to feel connected to a home he lost, Gamora’s yearning for redemption, and the simple, profound loyalty of Groot—these moments land with genuine force.

And oh, that soundtrack. The "Awesome Mix Vol. 1" isn't just background music; it's the film's soul. It’s the thread that connects a scruffy space captain to a small boy on Earth, turning "Hooked on a Feeling" and "Come and Get Your Love" into anthems of found family.

Guardians of the Galaxy is a vibrant, hilarious, and surprisingly tender reminder that we are all a little broken, but we don't have to be alone. It’s a big-budget spectacle that feels like a warm, cosmic hug, and that’s why it has a permanent spot in my heart.

The Lord of The Rings(Trilogy)


For me, The Lord of the Rings is more than a trilogy of films; it is a journey I return to, a world I inhabit. It’s the cinematic equivalent of a well-worn, beloved book—its edges frayed from countless visits, its landscapes feeling as familiar as home. Peter Jackson’s monumental achievement isn’t just in the epic scale, but in the profound intimacy he finds within it.

This isn't merely a story about hobbits, wizards, and elves. At its heart, it’s a story about the quiet, stubborn courage of ordinary people. It’s about the weight of a simple gold ring and the immense burden of hope. We don't follow untouchable superheroes; we follow a frightened hobbit from the Shire who wishes none of this had happened, a warrior king grappling with his lineage, and an elf and dwarf who discover friendship is stronger than ancient prejudice.

The trilogy’s true power lies in its moments of quiet humanity amidst the soaring battles. It's the heartbreaking grace of Sam carrying Frodo up the slopes of Mount Doom, whispering, "I can't carry it for you, but I can carry you." It's the warmth of Aragorn gently telling the hobbits, "You bow to no one." It's the sorrow in Arwen's choice and the loyalty of Samwise Gamgee, the true hero of the story. These are the moments that bring tears to my eyes every single time, because they speak to the best parts of us: loyalty, friendship, and the resilience of the human (and hobbit) spirit.

The painstaking craftsmanship—from the practical sets of Hobbiton to Howard Shore’s majestic, soul-stirring score—creates a world that feels utterly tangible and alive. It’s a place you believe in completely.

The Lord of the Rings trilogy is a timeless epic about finding the strength to face the darkness, both in the world and within ourselves, and it reminds us that even the smallest person can change the course of the future. It is, and will always be, my ultimate escape and my greatest comfort.
